Indications for Intravenous Albumin

Albumin, a naturally occurring protein produced by the liver, plays a crucial role in maintaining oncotic pressure and transporting various substances, including hormones, drugs, and ions, throughout the body. In clinical settings, intravenous albumin is used to manage a range of conditions associated with low albumin levels or those requiring volume expansion.

Hypoalbuminemia and Its Consequences

Hypoalbuminemia, or low serum albumin levels, can result from chronic liver disease, nephrotic syndrome, malnutrition, or acute medical conditions such as sepsis or burns. Low albumin levels can lead to decreased colloid oncotic pressure, resulting in fluid shifting from the intravascular space to the interstitial space, causing edema and worsening tissue perfusion. Intravenous albumin administration in patients with hypoalbuminemia helps restore oncotic pressure, facilitating the return of fluid into the blood vessels and improving circulation.

Management of Cirrhosis and Associated Complications

One of the most well-documented uses of intravenous albumin is in the management of cirrhosis, particularly in the prevention of complications such as spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) and hepatorenal syndrome. In patients with cirrhosis, albumin is used for paracentesis procedures to prevent circulatory dysfunction by maintaining blood volume and pressure. Following paracentesis, albumin infusion is recommended, especially when large volumes of fluid are removed, to prevent paracentesis-induced circulatory dysfunction.

In the context of hepatorenal syndrome, albumin combined with vasoconstrictors is used to improve renal function by expanding plasma volume and improving renal perfusion. Albumin is also employed as part of the treatment regimen for SBP, where it has been shown to decrease renal impairment and improve survival by counteracting the endotoxin-mediated reduction in effective blood volume.

Critical Care and Acute Settings

Intravenous albumin is also indicated in critical care settings, particularly for the resuscitation of patients with septic shock, burns, or other forms of shock where there is significant fluid loss or capillary leak. While crystalloids are primarily used for initial resuscitation, albumin may be preferred in cases where patients require large volumes of fluids and are at risk of developing fluid overload with crystalloids alone.

Surgical and Post-Surgical Care

Albumin is utilized in surgical and post-surgical settings to manage blood volume and promote adequate tissue perfusion, particularly during major surgeries where significant blood loss occurs or when patients have preexisting hypoalbuminemia. Its use helps stabilize blood volume and supports patient recovery by ensuring that nutrients and medications are effectively transported to body tissues.

Considerations and Precautions

Despite its benefits, the use of intravenous albumin must be judicious, guided by specific clinical indications and patient conditions. Albumin is a blood-derived product and should be used cautiously in patients with heart failure or severe anemia due to the potential risk of fluid overload. As research continues, further insights into the optimal use of albumin in different clinical scenarios are expected to enhance patient care practices.
