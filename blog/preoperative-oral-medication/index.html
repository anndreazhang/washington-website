Preoperative Oral Medication for Analgesia

The evolution of postoperative pain management has shifted significantly from reactive symptomatic relief toward the implementation of preemptive analgesic strategies. Administering oral analgesic medications before surgery exploits the window of opportunity to modulate the nociceptive cascade before it is initiated, potentially reducing the overall intensity and duration of postoperative pain.

Gabapentinoids

Preoperative gabapentin or pregabalin, administered at doses of 300–1200 mg approximately 1–2 hours before surgery, has demonstrated the ability to reduce 24-hour postoperative opioid consumption by 20% to 62%. These agents work primarily by blocking voltage-gated calcium channels on presynaptic terminals, reducing the release of excitatory neurotransmitters such as glutamate and substance P. Beyond their analgesic effects, gabapentinoids offer additional benefits including anxiolysis and a reduction in opioid-related side effects such as nausea, vomiting, and sedation. Clinicians should be mindful of dose-dependent adverse effects including dizziness and sedation, particularly in elderly patients.

COX-2 Inhibitors

Celecoxib, administered at 200–400 mg preoperatively 30–60 minutes before surgery, has demonstrated superior efficacy compared to other NSAIDs in reducing postoperative pain and morphine requirements. Its selective inhibition of cyclooxygenase-2 limits prostaglandin synthesis at the site of tissue injury while preserving COX-1-mediated gastroprotection. The preoperative window allows for maximum plasma concentrations to coincide with the inflammatory response initiated during surgical incision, offering a pharmacological advantage over postoperative administration.

Acetaminophen

Oral acetaminophen, when combined with NSAIDs, provides synergistic analgesic effects through complementary mechanisms. Evidence suggests that oral acetaminophen is as effective as the intravenous route when bioavailability is considered, offering a cost-effective alternative. Its favorable safety profile makes it suitable for most patients, and its inclusion in multimodal preoperative regimens is supported by numerous clinical guidelines.

Preoperative Opioids and Corticosteroids

Preoperative oral opioids show limited incremental benefit in opioid-naive patients and may increase the risk of respiratory depression in the perioperative period. Their routine use is not generally recommended in contemporary multimodal protocols. In contrast, corticosteroids such as dexamethasone offer additional analgesic and antiemetic advantages when administered preoperatively, reducing both pain scores and the incidence of postoperative nausea and vomiting through their anti-inflammatory and central mechanisms.

Clinical Implications

Effective preoperative analgesia requires individualization based on patient factors including age, comorbidities, baseline opioid tolerance, and the anticipated surgical stimulus. Standardized multimodal preoperative protocols incorporating gabapentinoids, COX-2 inhibitors, and acetaminophen have been shown to improve recovery trajectories, reduce postoperative opioid requirements, and facilitate earlier discharge in ambulatory surgical settings — all priorities central to modern perioperative care.
