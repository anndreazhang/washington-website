Safety Profile of Nonsteroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs) for Postoperative Analgesia

Clinical management of postoperative pain has undergone a paradigm shift toward multimodal analgesia approaches that minimize opioid dependence while maintaining effective pain control. NSAIDs have become central to these multimodal regimens, yet concerns about their safety profile have historically limited their use. A careful review of the evidence reveals that, for most patients, short-term NSAID use in the postoperative setting carries an acceptable risk profile.

Renal Function

Research indicates that short-term NSAID use causes transient and clinically insignificant effects on renal function in patients with normal baseline kidney function. Prostaglandins play a limited role in maintaining renal perfusion under normal hemodynamic conditions; however, they become critical in states of reduced effective circulating volume. Patients with pre-existing renal insufficiency, congestive heart failure, cirrhosis, or those receiving nephrotoxic agents require careful individual risk assessment before NSAIDs are administered.

Bleeding Risk

Analysis of data from over 11,000 patients demonstrated that ketorolac and similar non-selective NSAIDs do not meaningfully increase surgical site bleeding when used at recommended doses in the postoperative period. This finding contrasts with preoperative administration, where COX-1-mediated platelet inhibition poses a measurable bleeding risk, particularly in procedures where hemostasis is critical. The evidence supports postoperative rather than preoperative initiation in many cases.

Tissue and Bone Healing

Clinical evidence does not support the conclusion that brief postoperative NSAID courses — typically fewer than 7–10 days — meaningfully impair fracture healing in most orthopedic contexts. However, certain procedures warrant caution: rotator cuff repairs and spinal fusion surgeries have demonstrated sensitivity to COX-2-dependent prostaglandin signaling, which is involved in the bone remodeling cascade. For these specific procedures, selective COX-2 inhibitors may pose a greater concern than non-selective agents, and the risk-benefit discussion should be individualized.

Cardiovascular and Gastrointestinal Risk

Short NSAID courses of fewer than seven days are associated with minimal cardiovascular and gastrointestinal complications in otherwise healthy surgical patients. A notable exception is colorectal surgery, where evidence suggests an increased rate of anastomotic leakage with NSAID use — a finding that has led many colorectal surgical teams to restrict their routine use in this population. For the broader surgical population, the short duration of use substantially mitigates the risks associated with longer-term NSAID exposure.

Clinical Recommendations

NSAIDs should be routinely incorporated into multimodal postoperative analgesic regimens at the lowest effective dose and for the shortest necessary duration. Their use should be avoided in patients with severe renal impairment, active peptic ulcer disease, and in the specific high-risk contexts described above. When selected thoughtfully, NSAIDs offer meaningful opioid-sparing effects that contribute to improved patient recovery and reduced opioid-related adverse events.
